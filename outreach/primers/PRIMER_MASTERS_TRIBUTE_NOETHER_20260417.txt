EMMY NOETHER — SESSION FOCUS
================================================================================

Emmy Noether is Page I of the Masters Tribute. She is first because the
mathematics demands it: "Noether current" is the most-used technical term
in the entire Ainulindalë Conjecture body of work.

--- WHY SHE IS FIRST ---

The Noether current (𝒟_l J^{a,l} = 0) is the conservation law measured at
every stratum boundary in SMNNIP. It is the primary diagnostic — if ΔJ is
within tolerance, gauge symmetry is intact and information is conserved.
The neural Yang-Mills equation is a direct application of her theorem.
U(1)×SU(2)×SU(3) — the gauge group forced by Dixon's theorem — exists
as a concept because of Noether's second theorem on local symmetries.
Without her, there is no gauge theory. Without gauge theory, there is no
Standard Model. Without the Standard Model, the isomorphism has nothing
to be isomorphic to.

--- THE THEOREM (BOTH PARTS) ---

FIRST THEOREM (1915, published 1918):
  For every continuous global symmetry of the action of a physical system,
  there exists a corresponding conserved current.
  Time-translation → energy conservation
  Spatial-translation → momentum conservation
  Rotation → angular momentum conservation
  U(1) phase symmetry → electric charge conservation

SECOND THEOREM (same paper, less famous, more important):
  For every continuous local (gauge) symmetry, there exists a constraint
  on the equations of motion (Bianchi identity).
  This is the mathematical foundation of ALL gauge theories.
  Electromagnetism = U(1) gauge theory (from her second theorem)
  Weak force = SU(2) gauge theory (from her second theorem)
  Strong force = SU(3) gauge theory (from her second theorem)
  U(1)×SU(2)×SU(3) = Standard Model (all from her second theorem)

--- THE LIFE ---

1882: Born Erlangen, Bavaria. Father Max Noether, mathematician.
1904: Erlangen reverses policy on women students. She matriculates.
1907: Doctorate, summa cum laude. Under Paul Gordan.
1907-1915: Seven years at Erlangen. No pay. No title. Working and publishing.
1915: Hilbert and Klein invite her to Göttingen. Energy problem in GR.
      She solves it. Proves the theorem as a consequence.
1915-1919: At Göttingen, unable to obtain position. Teaching under
           Hilbert's name. Course listed as "Professor Hilbert, with the
           assistance of Dr. E. Noether."
           Hilbert: "We are a university, not a bathing establishment."
           He was overruled.
1919: Permitted to become Privatdozent after Weimar Republic reforms.
1922: "Honorary associate professor" — no salary, no security.
1923: First salary from the university. She is 41.
1933: Nazi dismissal letter, April. One of the first academics removed.
      Response: concern for students. No self-pity. She arranged what she
      could and looked for somewhere to go.
1933: Position at Bryn Mawr College, Pennsylvania. Women's college —
      one of the few institutions that could employ her without hesitation.
      Also lectured at Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton.
      Could not enter Princeton University library. Women not admitted.
1935: Died April 14, complications after surgery. Age 53.

Einstein, New York Times, April 1935:
  "In the judgment of the most competent living mathematicians, Fräulein
   Noether was the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far
   produced, since the higher education of women began."

Weyl, memorial address at Bryn Mawr:
  "She was not clay, pressed by the artistic hands of God into a harmonious
   form, but rather a chunk of human primary rock into which He had blown
   his creative breath of life."
