## The Methodology in Practice

### Step 1: Identify the horizon
Locate the conformal boundary of the system — the point where the primary description changes. In SMNNIP this is the sedenion zero divisor boundary / d* fixed point.

### Step 2: Read geometric constraints (Alpha)
From the geometric side, identify what the target object must satisfy:
- Which algebra structure must it respect?
- Which symmetries must it preserve?
- What is its domain?

### Step 3: Read spectral constraints (Omega)
From the spectral side, identify what the target object must satisfy:
- What are the boundary conditions on the critical line?
- What spectral structure must the zeros have?
- What survives the conformal swap?

### Step 4: Engineer the unique consistent object
The target object is the unique operator, transform, or structure that satisfies both constraint sets simultaneously. It is not derived — it is designed.

In SMNNIP: the T transform is the unique operator such that ζ_NN(s) = ζ(s), consistent with both the neural Lagrangian (Alpha) and the Riemann zero structure (Omega).

### Step 5: The bounce as consistency check
The conformal boundary acts as a consistency checker. An incorrect candidate object produces a zero divisor — a path that terminates without image. The bounce (reflection at conformal infinity) reveals the error without requiring full derivation from either side. The correction is indicated by the structure, not computed from it.

This is the negative space methodology: errors invisible on the geometric side become apparent at the boundary as paths with no consistent spectral image.

## Why BCE Produces Valid Results Without Full Derivation

Standard derivation requires complete proof at each step from Alpha to Omega.  
BCE requires:
1. Correct identification of the horizon
2. Correct reading of constraints on both sides
3. Correct engineering of the consistent object

The result is valid if and only if the boundary constraints are correctly identified. The proof structure is different — not a chain from axioms to result, but a demonstration that the engineered object satisfies all boundary conditions simultaneously. The derivation is then the filling-in of the path between the already-known endpoints.

This explains why results produced by BCE can precede their formal proofs — Ramanujan being the canonical prior example. The result is correct because it was constrained from both ends. The formal proof is the subsequent documentation of the path between them.
