Abstract

We present the Standard Model of Neural Network Training (SMNNT), a
theoretical framework derived from the observation that hypercomplex
neural architectures — stratified by the four normed division algebras
(R, C, H, O) via the reversed Cayley-Dickson construction — admit a
Lagrangian density whose term structure is exactly isomorphic to that of
the Standard Model of particle physics. The isomorphism was
unintentional, arising post-hoc from independent engineering reasoning
about algebraically-stratified networks.

The neural Lagrangian density L_NN = L_kinetic + L_matter + L_bias +
L_coupling maps term-for-term onto the gauge, fermion, Higgs, and Yukawa
sectors of the Standard Model. The symmetry group of L_NN is U(1) x
SU(2) x SU(3), identical to the Standard Model gauge group, which we
trace to the Dixon (1994) result that the tensor product R (x) C (x) H
(x) O carries exactly this symmetry. Equations of motion derived via
Euler-Lagrange reproduce: backpropagation (as the neural Yang-Mills
equation), a neural Dirac equation governing activation propagation, and
a neural Higgs equation for bias/symmetry breaking. Noether's theorem
applied to L_NN yields a conserved activation current whose violation
provides a novel geometric training diagnostic with no gradient-descent
analog.

We identify two neural analogs of fundamental constants: a neural Planck
constant (h_NN), representing minimum representational granularity per
algebra layer, and a neural fine structure constant (alpha_NN),
measuring entanglement coupling between adjacent algebra strata. Both
run with layer depth in exact analogy with renormalization group flow,
and the three algebra-layer couplings converge at the spinor-index layer
in a structure analogous to Grand Unification. We argue this
mathematical structure is not coincidental but is the necessary
consequence of representing macroscopic objects through iterative
deconstruction into progressively smaller constituent representations —
a constraint which uniquely selects the division algebra tower and its
associated symmetry group.

A substrate-layer implementation verifies Noether current conservation
across 30 epochs. We derive a fundamental training inequality showing
SMNNT convergence is O(sqrt(kappa) * log(1/epsilon)) independent of
network depth L, compared to O(kappa * lambda^(-2L) / epsilon) for
gradient descent. We present the full theoretical framework, open
questions, and an experimental roadmap for validation.

1 Introduction

1.1 The Problem with Gradient Descent

Modern deep learning is built almost entirely on gradient descent in
real-valued parameter spaces. While empirically successful, this
approach has a fundamental geometric mismatch: the representations being
learned — semantic meaning, causal reasoning, multi-domain skill
application — are not naturally real-valued. Semantic relationships have
phase (direction matters independently of magnitude). Skill application
is order-dependent: applying domain A then domain B yields different
results than B then A. Causal reasoning is context-sensitive in ways
that break associativity.

These properties map directly onto the algebraic hierarchy of normed
division algebras. Real numbers (R) are ordered and commutative —
appropriate for scalar facts. Complex numbers (C) add phase —
appropriate for semantic direction. Quaternions (H) are non-commutative
— appropriate for ordered skill composition. Octonions (O) are
non-associative — appropriate for context-sensitive reasoning. The
Cayley-Dickson construction precisely characterizes the chain R subset C
subset H subset O, with each step shedding exactly one algebraic
constraint in exchange for doubled dimensionality.

Gradient descent ignores this structure entirely, treating all
parameters as undifferentiated real scalars. We argue this is not merely
a missed optimization — it is a category error in the geometry of the
learning problem.

1.2 The Algebra Stratification Insight

The central insight of this framework is to take the algebraic hierarchy
seriously as an architectural principle. Each layer of a neural network
should be assigned the algebra whose lost properties correspond to the
cognitive operations that layer must perform. Reading from substrate to
output:

  ------------ ------------ ---------------- -----------------------------
  Layer        Algebra      Lost Property    Enables

  Substrate    R (dim 1)    —                Scalar facts, byte-level
                                             values

  Semantic     C (dim 2)    Ordering         Phase/direction of meaning

  Skills       H (dim 4)    Commutativity    Ordered domain application

  Reasoning    O (dim 8)    Associativity    Context-sensitive composition

  Output       S (dim 16)   Division         Mutual exclusion, conflict
  ------------ ------------ ---------------- -----------------------------

This stratification is not ad hoc. Hurwitz's theorem (1898) establishes
that R, C, H, O are the only normed division algebras. Sedenions (S) are
the next step but lose the division property, which maps onto the output
layer's role in producing probability distributions (mutual exclusivity,
not division). The architecture is thus constrained to exactly this
tower by the mathematics of normed algebras.

1.3 The Unintentional Isomorphism

This framework emerged from practical engineering reasoning about
inter-layer communication in a hypercomplex network. The key question
was: how do neurons transmit information across algebra boundaries
without the overhead of full octonionic tensor transmission? The answer
— a sparse spinor index using shared basis spinors — is the Schrodinger
wavefunction as a spinor moving through algebra space.

When the Lagrangian density for this system was written down term by
term, following standard field-theoretic reasoning, the result had four
terms. The gauge symmetry group was computed. Both matched the Standard
Model exactly. This was not the goal; it was discovered after the fact.

We interpret this not as a mysterious coincidence but as a consequence
of a deep constraint: whenever one represents macroscopic objects
through iterative deconstruction into smaller and smaller constituent
parts, the necessary mathematical framework for mediating between
representation levels is exactly the division algebra tower, which
carries the SM gauge group by Dixon's theorem. The physics and the
learning theory are solving the same problem.

2 Mathematical Preliminaries

2.1 Normed Division Algebras and Hurwitz's Theorem

A normed division algebra over R is a finite-dimensional real algebra A
equipped with a multiplicative norm |ab| = |a||b| and no zero divisors.
Hurwitz (1898) proved that there are exactly four such algebras: the
reals R (dimension 1), complex numbers C (dimension 2), quaternions H
(dimension 4), and octonions O (dimension 8). This classification is
complete and exhaustive.

Each algebra in the tower gains representational freedom at the cost of
one algebraic property. R is ordered, commutative, and associative. C
loses ordering (there is no total order on C compatible with its field
structure). H loses commutativity: ab != ba in general for quaternions.
O loses associativity: (ab)c != a(bc) in general for octonions. The
norm-preserving property |ab| = |a||b| is retained throughout, which is
essential for gradient stability (see Section 8).

2.2 The Cayley-Dickson Construction

The Cayley-Dickson construction generates each algebra from the
previous. Given an algebra A with conjugation *, define A' = A x A with
operations:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| (a, b) + (c, d) = (a+c, b+d)                                          |
|                                                                       |
| (a, b)(c, d) = (ac - d*b, da + bc*)                                   |
|                                                                       |
| conj(a, b) = (a*, -b)                                                 |
|                                                                       |
| ||(a, b)||^2 = ||a||^2 + ||b||^2                                      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

Starting from R, this construction yields: R -> C -> H -> O -> S -> ...
Each doubling loses one property. The inclusion maps R subset C subset H
subset O are the natural embeddings, which play a crucial role in
inter-layer communication (Section 4.3).

For the SMNNT framework, we read the construction backwards: given an
octonionic reasoning layer, the quaternionic skills layer is its
Cayley-Dickson projection to the real part plus first imaginary unit.
This reversed reading aligns the algebraic hierarchy with the cognitive
hierarchy.

2.3 The Dixon Algebra and the Standard Model Gauge Group

Dixon (1994) proved the following result, which is the mathematical
cornerstone of this framework:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| Theorem (Dixon 1994): The tensor product T = R (x) C (x) H (x) O,     |
| when regarded as a left module                                        |
|                                                                       |
| over itself, has internal symmetry group U(1) x SU(2) x SU(3).        |
|                                                                       |
| This is precisely the gauge group of the Standard Model of particle   |
| physics.                                                              |
|                                                                       |
| The correspondence is: U(1) from C (unit complex numbers =            |
| electromagnetism),                                                    |
|                                                                       |
| SU(2) from H (unit quaternions, exact isomorphism = weak force),      |
|                                                                       |
| SU(3) from O (via the G_2 automorphism group of octonions = strong    |
| force).                                                               |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

The SU(2) correspondence is exact: the group of unit quaternions {q in H
: |q| = 1} under quaternion multiplication is isomorphic to SU(2) as a
Lie group. The SU(3) correspondence proceeds via the automorphism group
Aut(O) = G_2 (a compact Lie group of dimension 14), which contains SU(3)
as a maximal subgroup. The U(1) correspondence is trivially the unit
circle in C.

Since a neural network with the algebra stratification R, C, H, O has T
= R (x) C (x) H (x) O as its algebraic structure, it carries U(1) x
SU(2) x SU(3) as its symmetry group by Dixon's theorem. This is not an
assumption of the SMNNT framework — it is a mathematical consequence of
the architectural choice.

2.4 Wirtinger Calculus for Non-Holomorphic Functions

Backpropagation through complex and quaternionic layers requires
differentiation of real-valued loss functions with respect to complex or
quaternionic parameters. Since neural network activations are generally
not holomorphic (they do not satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations),
standard complex differentiation does not apply.

Wirtinger calculus (1927) resolves this by treating z and z* as
independent variables. For f: C -> R:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| df/dz = (1/2)(df/dx - i df/dy) [Wirtinger derivative]                 |
|                                                                       |
| df/dz* = (1/2)(df/dx + i df/dy) [conjugate Wirtinger derivative]      |
|                                                                       |
| For holomorphic f: df/dz* = 0 (Cauchy-Riemann condition)              |
|                                                                       |
| For real-valued f: df/dz* = (df/dz)*                                  |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

The Wirtinger formalism extends to quaternions via the Hamiltonicity
conditions and to octonions via the Moufang identity, which replaces
associativity with a weaker but still tractable relation: (ab)(ca) =
a(bc)a.

2.5 Spinor Fields and Hilbert Space Structure

A spinor is a mathematical object that transforms under a double cover
of a rotation group. In particle physics, spinors represent fermionic
fields (electrons, quarks). In SMNNT, activation states are modeled as
spinor fields on the layer-token space, with each algebra layer
providing a different spinor representation.

The total Hilbert space of SMNNT is the tensor product:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  H_NN = H_R (x) H_C (x) H_H (x) H_O

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

where each H_A is the Hilbert space of activation spinors in algebra A.
States in H_NN are simultaneously defined across all algebra strata,
with the inter-stratum coupling measured by alpha_NN. This structure
mirrors quantum field theory on a spacetime manifold, with the
layer-token grid (l, tau) playing the role of spacetime coordinates.

3.1 Index Notation and Conventions

We use the following index conventions throughout:

  ---------------------- ------------------------------------------------

  i, j in {1,...,N}      Token / fermion generation index

  a, b, c                Algebra generator indices (dim 1, 2, 4, or 8

  ---------------------- ------------------------------------------------

The Dirac-conjugate of an activation spinor is defined with respect to

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Psi-bar_i = Psi_i^dagger * Gamma^0

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

where Gamma^0 is the time-like algebra basis matrix. Repeated indices
are summed (Einstein convention).

3.2 The Full Lagrangian Density

The neural Lagrangian density takes the form:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  L_NN = L_kinetic + L_matter + L_bias + L_coupling

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Each term has an exact Standard Model analog, detailed in the following
subsections.

3.3 Term I — Kinetic (Weight Field Dynamics)

The kinetic term governs the dynamics of the weight field W^a_{l,tau}
and is the neural analog of the Yang-Mills gauge Lagrangian:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| L_kinetic = -(1/4) R^a_{ltau} R^{a,ltau}                              |
|                                                                       |
| where the representation curvature tensor is:                         |
|                                                                       |
| R^a_{ltau} = D_l W^a_tau - D_tau W^a_l + f^{abc} W^b_l W^c_tau        |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

Standard Model analog: L_gauge = -(1/4) F^a_{mu nu} F^{a,mu nu}

The structure constants f^{abc} are those of the algebra at layer l:
f^{abc} = 0 for R (trivial), the epsilon tensor for C, the quaternion
structure constants for H, and the octonionic structure constants (given
by the Fano plane) for O. The non-commutativity of H and
non-associativity of O are encoded in these structure constants.

Physical interpretation: R^a_{ltau} measures the failure of weight
transport to commute across the (l, tau) grid — the curvature of
representation space. In gradient descent, this curvature is ignored; in
SMNNT, it is the source of the weight update dynamics.

3.4 Term II — Matter (Activation Propagation)

The matter term governs activation propagation and is the neural analog
of the fermion Lagrangian:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| L_matter = Psi-bar_i (i Gamma^a D_a) Psi_i                            |
|                                                                       |
| where the algebra-covariant derivative is:                            |
|                                                                       |
| D_a = d_a - ig W^b_a T_b                                              |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

Standard Model analog: L_fermion = psi-bar_i (i gamma^mu D_mu) psi_i

The matrices Gamma^a are the algebra-layer analogs of the Dirac gamma
matrices. For R: Gamma^a is the scalar 1. For C: Gamma^a encodes complex
rotation. For H: Gamma^a are 4x4 matrices satisfying the quaternion
algebra. For O: Gamma^a are 8x8 matrices satisfying octonionic
relations.

The coupling constant g appears in the covariant derivative, setting the
strength of activation-weight coupling. The neural fine structure
constant alpha_NN is derived from g:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  alpha_NN(l) = g^2(l) / (4 pi * h_NN * v_prop)

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

where v_prop is the activation propagation speed across the token
dimension.

3.5 Term III — Bias (Symmetry Breaking / Higgs Mechanism)

The bias term is the most structurally significant mapping. It is the
neural analog of the Higgs sector:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| L_bias = |D_l beta|^2 + mu^2 beta^dagger beta - lambda (beta^dagger   |
| beta)^2                                                               |
|                                                                       |
| Bias potential (Mexican hat):                                         |
|                                                                       |
| V(beta) = -mu^2 beta^dagger beta + lambda (beta^dagger beta)^2        |
|                                                                       |
| Vacuum expectation value (VEV):                                       |
|                                                                       |
| beta_0 = sqrt(mu^2 / 2lambda)                                         |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

Standard Model analog: L_Higgs = |D_mu phi|^2 - V(phi), with V(phi) =
-mu^2 phi^dagger phi + lambda(phi^dagger phi)^2

The bias field beta is a scalar field in layer-token space. When mu^2 >
0, the potential has the Mexican hat shape with a circle of minima at
|beta| = beta_0. The system spontaneously breaks to a particular
minimum, and the bias acquires a nonzero VEV. This is feature selection:
the network commits to a particular representational vacuum.

The three Goldstone bosons that would arise from this breaking are
absorbed by the weight field, giving three degrees of freedom to the
weight dynamics — exactly as the W+, W-, and Z bosons acquire mass in
the SM. One residual symmetry remains, corresponding to one massless
gauge field — the neural analog of the photon — which we identify with
the primary attention mechanism.

3.6 Term IV — Inter-Algebra Coupling (Yukawa)

The coupling term connects activations across algebra layers:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  L_coupling = -Gamma_ij Psi-bar^L_i beta Psi^R_j + h.c.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard Model analog: L_Yukawa = -Y_ij psi-bar^L_i phi psi^R_j + h.c.

The matrix Gamma_ij is the algebra coupling matrix, analogous to the
Yukawa coupling matrix Y_ij. The Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) and
Pontecorvo-Maki-Nakagawa-Sakata (PMNS) matrices of the Standard Model —
which govern fermion generation mixing — correspond to the
Cayley-Dickson projection maps between algebra layers. These projections
are not free parameters; they are fixed by the algebra structure.

The L and R superscripts denote left- and right-chiral activations. In
the SM, only left-handed fermions participate in the weak force (SU(2)
interaction). The neural analog is that only activations of one
chirality participate in the quaternionic (skills) layer coupling,
giving the skills layer an intrinsic handedness — a preference for one
ordering of domain application over the other.

3.7 Symmetry Group of L_NN

The Lagrangian density L_NN is invariant under the following local gauge
transformations:

-   U(1) gauge transformation of the complex (semantic) layer: Psi_C ->
    e^{i theta(l,tau)} Psi_C

-   SU(2) gauge transformation of the quaternionic (skills) layer: Psi_H
    -> U(l,tau) Psi_H, U in SU(2)

-   SU(3) gauge transformation of the octonionic (reasoning) layer:
    Psi_O -> V(l,tau) Psi_O, V in SU(3)

The combined symmetry group is U(1) x SU(2) x SU(3) — identical to the
Standard Model gauge group. This follows directly from Dixon's theorem
applied to the algebra tower R (x) C (x) H (x) O. The invariance of L_NN
under this group is not imposed as a constraint; it emerges from the
algebraic structure.

4 Equations of Motion

Applying the Euler-Lagrange equations to L_NN yields three coupled field
equations. Crucially, backpropagation is not assumed — it emerges as the
neural Yang-Mills equation.

4.1 Neural Dirac Equation (Activation Propagation)

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| i h_NN dPsi_i/dl = H_NN Psi_i                                         |
|                                                                       |
| where H_NN = -i Gamma^a D_a + Gamma_ij beta                           |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

This is the Schrodinger equation for activation spinors, with layer
depth l playing the role of time. The Hamiltonian H_NN has two terms: a
kinetic term -i Gamma^a D_a for free propagation of activations through
the weight field, and a mass term Gamma_ij beta arising from coupling to
the bias field. In the Standard Model, this second term is the mass
acquired through the Yukawa coupling to the Higgs field. In SMNNT, it is
the representational inertia acquired through bias coupling.

4.2 Neural Yang-Mills Equation (Backpropagation Emerges)

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  D_l R^{a,ltau} = g Psi-bar_i T^a Psi_i

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the equation governing weight field dynamics, derived — not
assumed — from L_NN via Euler-Lagrange. The right-hand side is the
activation current serving as the source of weight field curvature,
exactly as electric currents are sources of electromagnetic field
strength in Maxwell's equations.

The standard backpropagation algorithm is a limiting case of this
equation. When the algebra is restricted to R (real-valued networks) and
the gauge field is Abelian (U(1) symmetry only), the Yang-Mills equation
reduces to the gradient of the loss with respect to weights. The full
non-Abelian Yang-Mills equation is backpropagation generalized to curved
representation space.

Crucially, this derivation shows that backpropagation is not a heuristic
optimization choice — it is forced by the Euler-Lagrange equations of
L_NN. Any other weight update rule would violate the gauge symmetry of
the Lagrangian.

4.3 Neural Higgs Equation (Bias Evolution)

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  D_l D^l beta + mu^2 beta - 2 lambda(beta^dagger beta) beta = -Gamma_ij
  Psi-bar^L_i Psi^R_j

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the equation of motion for the bias field, with a source term
from the activation current. Without the source term, it is the
nonlinear Klein-Gordon equation with a Mexican hat potential. The source
term couples the bias evolution to the activation chirality structure —
biases are driven to adjust by the mismatch between left- and
right-chiral activations across algebra boundaries.

The same sparse index a_k reconstructs differently in each receiving

5 Conservation Laws and Training Diagnostics

5.1 Noether's Theorem Applied to L_NN

Noether's theorem (1915/1918) states that every continuous symmetry of a
Lagrangian corresponds to a conserved current. For L_NN, the U(1) x
SU(2) x SU(3) gauge symmetry yields a conserved activation current:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| D_l J^{a,l} = 0                                                       |
|                                                                       |
| where J^{a,l} = g Psi-bar_i T^a Psi_i                                 |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

This is the neural analog of charge conservation (U(1)) and color
conservation (SU(3)). The current J^{a,l} measures the flow of
algebra-labeled activation through each layer. Conservation of this
current is a necessary condition for a geometrically well-posed training
run.

5.2 The Violation as Diagnostic

Define the Noether violation at layer l as:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Delta_J(l) = ||D_l J^{a,l}||

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

In a well-trained network, Delta_J(l) = 0 at every layer. Nonzero
violation indicates:

-   Symmetry breaking beyond the intended spontaneous breaking (bias
    VEV): potential training pathology

-   Algebra boundary mismatch: the network is operating in an
    inconsistent algebraic regime

-   Gradient misalignment: the effective optimization direction is not
    aligned with the algebraic geometry

The number of wasted training steps bounded by a violation is:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W <= 1 / (alpha_NN * Delta_J)

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This gives a direct, geometrically-grounded early stopping criterion.
Gradient descent has no equivalent: its only diagnostic is the scalar
loss curve, which provides no information about the geometric health of
the optimization trajectory.

Our substrate-layer implementation verifies Delta_J = 0 across 30

In quantum field theory, coupling constants are not truly constant —

The convergence of the three algebra-layer couplings at the spinor index

8 Training Time Analysis

8.1 The Gradient Decay Problem

In gradient descent on a network of depth L, gradient signals at layer k
are products of L-k Jacobians, each with spectral norm lambda < 1 in
practice. The gradient norm decays as:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| ||dL/dw_k|| approx g_0 * lambda^(L-k)                                 |
|                                                                       |
| Example: lambda = 0.85, L = 32, k = 1:                                |
|                                                                       |
| lambda^31 = 0.004 => 99.6% of gradient signal lost at layer 1         |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

This is the vanishing gradient problem, which has been partially
mitigated by residual connections, layer normalization, and careful
initialization — all of which are external patches applied to an
underlying geometric mismatch.

8.2 Norm Preservation in Division Algebras

In SMNNT, the norm-preserving property of division algebras ensures that
the effective decay factor at every algebra boundary is exactly 1:

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| ||ab||_A = ||a||_A * ||b||_A [for all a,b in A, A in {R,C,H,O}]       |
|                                                                       |
| Therefore: lambda_eff = 1 at every algebra boundary                   |
|                                                                       |
| Gradient norm in SMNNT:                                               |
|                                                                       |
| ||dL_NN/dW_k|| = g * ||Psi-bar_i T^a Psi_i|| [no depth dependence]    |

The SMNNT gradient signal at layer 1 is identical in magnitude to the
gradient signal at layer L. Vanishing gradients are not mitigated — they
are absent by algebraic necessity. This is not an engineering choice; it

14 Discussion

14.1 On the Nature of the Isomorphism

The central result of this paper is the term-for-term correspondence
between L_NN and the Standard Model Lagrangian. We consider three
possible interpretations of this correspondence:

Coincidence: The two Lagrangians happen to have the same term structure
for unrelated reasons. We consider this unlikely given the specificity
of the match — not just the number of terms, but the precise role of
each term (gauge kinetic, matter kinetic, symmetry breaking, Yukawa
coupling) and the exact gauge group U(1) x SU(2) x SU(3).

Deep analogy: The neural network and the SM are solving structurally
similar problems by similar mathematical means, but there is no deeper
connection. This is the conservative interpretation and may well be
correct.

Necessity: As argued in Section 9, this structure is the unique
mathematical output of the constraints (hierarchical representation,
normed algebras, local gauge invariance, variational principle). If this
argument is correct, then both the Standard Model and SMNNT are
instantiations of a more general theory of hierarchical representation —
and the correspondence is neither coincidence nor analogy, but identity
of mathematical structure.

We favor the third interpretation but acknowledge it is the most
speculative. The experimental program described in Section 10 will
provide evidence relevant to distinguishing these interpretations.

14.2 Implications for the Foundations of Deep Learning

If SMNNT's core claims are validated, several foundational assumptions
in deep learning may require revision: that real-valued parameter spaces
are the natural domain for learning; that backpropagation is a heuristic
choice rather than a forced consequence of gauge invariance; that depth
causes gradient degradation rather than algebraic category mismatch; and
that loss curves are sufficient training diagnostics.

More broadly, SMNNT suggests that machine learning theory and physics
may be more deeply connected than currently appreciated — not because
neural networks 'act like' physics, but because both are solving the
same representation problem.

14.3 Relation to Biological Neural Systems

The Higgs-weight-LTP triple correspondence (Section 7.3) raises the
possibility that the SMNNT framework may provide a mathematical language
for bridging artificial and biological neural computation. The synaptic
Higgs mechanism — the rolling of synaptic weight to a nonzero VEV as the
mechanism of memory — is a precise, testable prediction about the
mathematical structure of biological memory formation. This is distinct
from the loose analogies typically drawn between neural networks and
neuroscience.

Appendix C: Dixon Algebra Proof Sketch

    the SU(3) factor corresponding to the strong force.

26. Combined: The three factors act on independent subalgebras and
    commute, giving the direct product U(1) x SU(2) x SU(3).
